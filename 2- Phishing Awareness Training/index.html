What is Phishing?
Phishing is a cyberattack where criminals impersonate trusted organizations via email, text messages, or fake websites to trick you into revealing sensitive information like passwords, credit card numbers, or personal data.

Types of Phishing Attacks

Attackers use multiple channels to reach their victims

📧

Email Phishing

The most common type. Mass emails pretending to be from banks, services, or companies asking you to click links or provide credentials.

📱

SMS Phishing (Smishing)

Fake text messages with urgent alerts like "Your package delivery failed" or "Your account is suspended" with malicious links.

📞

Voice Phishing (Vishing)

Phone calls from scammers pretending to be tech support, banks, or government agencies like the IRS or Social Security.

🎯

Spear Phishing

Highly targeted attacks aimed at specific individuals or companies, using personal information to appear more convincing.

📋

Clone Phishing

Attackers duplicate a legitimate email you received, replace real links with malicious ones, and resend it from a spoofed address.

💼

Whaling

Spear phishing targeted at high-profile individuals like CEOs, CFOs, or other executives with access to sensitive data.

How to Recognize Phishing Emails

Train your eye to spot the warning signs

Simulated Phishing Email
From: security@paypa1-secure.com
To: you@example.com
Subject: URGENT: Your Account Has Been Compromised!
Dear Customer,

We have detected unauthorized access to your account. Your account will be suspended within 24 hours unless you verify your identity immediately.

Click here to verify: https://paypa1-secure.com/verify?id=38291

If you do not respond, your account will be permanently locked.

Security Team
PayPal Inc.

Red Flags in This Email

  • FAKE SENDER Domain is "paypa1-secure.com" (note the number 1 instead of "l")
  • URGENCY "suspended within 24 hours" — pressure tactics to make you act fast
  • THREATS "permanently locked" — fear-based language
  • SUSPICIOUS LINK URL doesn't match the real paypal.com domain
  • GENERIC GREETING "Dear Customer" instead of your actual name
  • GRAMMAR Subtle inconsistencies in formatting and tone
Quick Check

Always hover over links before clicking to see the actual URL. If it doesn't match the official domain, do not click.

How to Spot Fake Websites

Scammers create convincing copies of real sites

Feature Legitimate Website Fake Website
URL https://www.paypal.com https://www.paypa1.com
SSL Certificate Valid, issued to correct org Expired, self-signed, or wrong org
Design Quality Professional, consistent Slightly off, low-res logos
Forms Only ask for necessary info Ask for SSN, full credit card, PIN
Spelling Proper spelling and grammar Typos, awkward phrasing

Common URL Tricks Used by Attackers

Typosquatting

g00gle.com or microsft.com — misspelled domains that look similar at a glance.

Subdomain Tricks

paypal.malicious-site.com — "paypal" is just a subdomain, the real site is malicious-site.com.

Homograph Attacks

Using special Unicode characters that look like Latin letters, e.g., using a Cyrillic "а" instead of "a".

Stay Safe

Always type website addresses directly into your browser instead of clicking links. Use a password manager — it won't autofill on fake sites.

Social Engineering Tactics

Attackers exploit human psychology, not just technology

⏱️

Urgency & Scarcity

"Act now! Offer expires in 1 hour!" — Forces you to act without thinking.

👔

Authority

Impersonating your CEO, bank manager, or the IRS to make you comply without questioning.

😱

Fear & Intimidation

"Your account will be closed!" or "Legal action will be taken!" — panic leads to mistakes.

🎁

Baiting

"You've won a free iPhone!" or infected USB drives left in parking lots to tempt curiosity.

🤝

Pretexting

Creating a fabricated scenario (fake IT support call) to gain your trust and extract information.

💬

Social Proof

"1,000 people have already claimed this offer!" — Using fake numbers to make attacks seem legitimate.

Remember: Social engineering works because it targets emotions, not systems. The strongest firewall cannot protect you if you willingly hand over your credentials. You are the last line of defense.

Real-World Phishing Examples

These attacks actually happened — and they cost millions

Case Study 1 — Google & Facebook ($100M+ Scam)

The Attack

From 2013 to 2015, Lithuanian attacker Evaldas Rimasauskas impersonated Quanta Computer (a legitimate hardware vendor) and sent phishing invoices to Google and Facebook employees.

The Result

Over $100 million was transferred to the attacker's accounts before the scheme was discovered. Both companies recovered most of the funds.

The Lesson

Always verify invoices with known vendor contacts through a separate, trusted channel.

Case Study 2 — Sony Pictures Entertainment (2014)

The Attack

Attackers sent spear phishing emails to Sony employees posing as Apple ID verification requests. Employees entered credentials on a fake login page.

The Result

The attackers gained access to Sony's internal network, leaked unreleased movies, personal employee data, and caused an estimated $100 million in damages.

The Lesson

Never enter credentials from a link in an email. Go directly to the service's official website.

Case Study 3 — Twitter CEO Jack Dorsey (2019)

The Attack

Attackers used a SMS-based phishing (smishing) attack to compromise the Twitter CEO's phone number through a SIM swap, then posted from his account.

The Result

Unauthorized tweets were sent from the CEO's account to his 4 million followers, damaging company reputation.

The Lesson

Enable PIN protection on your SIM card and use app-based two-factor authentication instead of SMS.

Best Practices & Tips

Follow these steps to protect yourself and your organization

Before You Click

  • Verify the sender — Check the email address carefully, not just the display name.
  • Hover over links — Preview the actual URL before clicking.
  • When in doubt, throw it out — If an email feels suspicious, delete it.
  • Don't download unexpected attachments — Especially .exe, .zip, or .docm files.

Strong Authentication

  • Use Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA) — Even if your password is stolen, MFA blocks unauthorized access.
  • Use a password manager — Creates and stores unique, strong passwords for every account.
  • Never reuse passwords — One compromised password shouldn't give access to all your accounts.

If You Suspect a Phishing Attempt

  1. Do not reply or click any links in the message.
  2. Report it — Forward phishing emails to your IT department or reportphishing@apwg.org.
  3. Delete the email after reporting it.
  4. If you clicked a link — Change your passwords immediately and scan your device for malware.

Key Takeaways

Remember these points to stay safe online

🔍

Inspect Before You Click

Check sender addresses, hover over links, and look for red flags like urgency, threats, and typos.

🔒

Secure Your Accounts

Use MFA, unique passwords via a password manager, and keep all software updated.

🧠

Think Before You Act

Attackers exploit emotions. Take a moment to verify before responding to any urgent request.

📢

Report Suspicious Activity

Forward phishing attempts to IT/security teams. Reporting protects everyone in your organization.
